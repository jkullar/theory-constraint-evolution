# Model — Age Compression

## Core Idea

Ages are getting shorter. Not randomly — by a compounding mechanism.

> Each age's solution leaves behind more capability (tools, population, coordination, communication speed), which gets applied to the next constraint. More accumulated capability = faster solution = shorter age.

This is a civilizational analog of Moore's Law.

---

## Observed Durations

| Age | Approx. Duration |
|---|---|
| Survival | ~190,000 years |
| Agriculture | ~7,000 years |
| Civilization | ~4,750 years |
| Industrial | ~150 years |
| Energy | ~70 years |
| Information | ~50 years |
| Alignment | ~5 years (ongoing) |

The compression ratio between ages is roughly **10–30x each time**.

---

## What Determines the Length of an Age

Three factors set how long a constraint dominates:

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Constraint difficulty** | How technically and socially hard is the solution? Harder = longer age |
| **Diffusion speed** | How fast does the solution spread to enough people to flip the dominant bottleneck? Faster = shorter age |
| **Accumulated capability** | How many tools, people, and coordination systems exist to attack the constraint? More = shorter age |

Accumulated capability is the compounding factor. Each solved age deposits:
- Better tools
- Larger population
- Faster communication
- Richer coordination systems

All of which get aimed at the next constraint. The deposit grows with every age — so the next age is attacked with more firepower than the last.

---

## The Compression Model

```
age_length ∝ 1 / cumulative_capability
```

As capability accumulates exponentially across ages, age length compresses exponentially.

## What Determines Transition Length

The **transition** is the overlap period where:
- The old constraint is solved enough to no longer be the dominant bottleneck
- The new constraint is emerging but not yet clearly named

Transition length is also shrinking, for the same reason: better communication and more minds means new constraints get *identified* faster.

However, transitions have their own floor — some changes require physical infrastructure build-out or generational behavior shift regardless of communication speed.

---

## The Lower Bound: Why Ages Cannot Compress to Zero

There is a minimum age length — a floor set by:

1. **Physical diffusion** — even with instant communication, some solutions require building physical infrastructure (power grids, factories, hardware) which takes real time
2. **Behavioral adoption** — new skills and mental models require generational uptake; you cannot teach the whole world a new way of thinking overnight
3. **Coordination lag** — large populations coordinating toward a new constraint take time to align, even when the constraint is visible

This means ages compress toward a lower bound, not to zero. That floor is probably measured in **years to a decade**, not months.

---

## Implications

**1. Current age is compressing faster than the timeline suggests**

The Alignment Age may resolve — or transition — within years, not the decades the timeline framing implies. Watch for when "aligned execution" stops being the bottleneck.

**2. Future predictions should use shorter time horizons**

Stage 1 (~10 years) and Stage 2 (~20–30 years) in predictions may be optimistic. The compression trend suggests Stage 1 could close faster.

**3. The gap between constraint identification and solution is shrinking**

In the Survival Age, it took thousands of years to spread fire broadly. In the Information Age, a solution (e.g. the smartphone) reached billions in under a decade. In the Alignment Age, solutions may reach scale in years.

**4. Individual positioning windows are shortening**

If ages compress, the window to learn the emerging skill, acquire the emerging asset, or build the emerging tool is narrowing. Early movers gain more advantage when the age is short — because latecomers have less time to catch up before the constraint flips again.

---

## Open Questions

- Is the compression ratio stable (~10–30x), or is it itself accelerating?
- Does the lower bound shift as physical infrastructure becomes faster to deploy (e.g. software-defined everything)?
- At what point does age length drop below the time required for skill acquisition — making continuous learning the only viable strategy?
- Can an age be *skipped* — where a constraint is solved before it fully dominates?
