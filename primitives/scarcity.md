# Primitive — Scarcity

## Definition

Scarcity is the dominant bottleneck of an age — the one thing whose absence limits everything else.

---

## Properties of a True Age-Defining Constraint

For a scarcity to define an age, it must be:

1. **Universal** — affects everyone, not just some
2. **Hard bottleneck** — limits output regardless of individual skill
3. **System-level** — not a personal weakness; a structural ceiling

### Examples that qualify:
- Energy in the industrial era (no power = no production)
- Land in agriculture (no land = no food)

### Examples that do NOT qualify as age-defining:
- Attention → manageable, trainable, not universal
- Intelligence → increasingly abundant via AI, not strictly scarce

---

## The Scarcity Progression

Scarcities evolve from **external/physical** → **internal/systemic**:

| Phase | Nature | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Early ages | Physical | Food, land, energy |
| Middle ages | Mechanical/infrastructural | Labor, power distribution |
| Modern | Digital | Information |
| Current | Systemic/human | Aligned execution |
| Future | Abstract | Meaning, direction |

---

## Key Rule

> Old scarcities never disappear.
> They become cheaper, more reliable, and less dominant.

Food is still important — it no longer defines civilization.

---

## Relationship to Other Primitives

- Scarcity defines the challenge
- **Builders** remove the scarcity
- **Scale** from their solution creates complexity
- **People Movers** restore coordination
- The loop reveals the **next scarcity**
